# Title
The title is the first element a reader encounters on a chart. It anchors the visual, conveys the main message, and sets the interpretive frame before any data is read. A well-written title reduces cognitive load by telling the reader what to look for; a vague or missing title forces them to reverse-engineer the chart's purpose from the data alone.
 
At the FSO, chart titles serve both communicative and institutional functions: they must be informative enough to stand alone in a report or slide deck, and consistent enough to meet the standards of official statistical communication.

## Description
A chart title should be a short, self-contained statement that communicates the chart's main takeaway or its subject matter, depending on context.
 
Two titling strategies are valid depending on the publication context:
 
- **Descriptive title**: names the topic and the variables shown, without interpreting the data. Suited for publications where the analyst role is neutral and interpretation is left to the reader (*"Employment rate by canton, 2023"*).
- **Informative (assertive) title**: states the main finding directly. Suited for editorial contexts, dashboards, or presentations where the author guides the reader's attention (*"Employment rates are highest in urban cantons"*).
Both approaches are acceptable within the FSO guidelines, but they should not be mixed within the same publication. The choice of strategy must be made consistently at the document level.
 
Regardless of strategy, a title should answer at minimum: **what** is being measured, and **when** or **where** it applies (if relevant). It should not duplicate information already available in the axis labels or caption.
